\begin{abstract}
Companies do not need another stateless analytics chatbot. They need a persistent analytical operating system that sits over the company's evolving memory, learns how the business defines and analyzes itself, and automates the complete data-analysis loop from request or signal to verified deliverable. This company memory spans data and schemas, metrics and business ontology, policies and authority, prior incidents and decisions, reviewer corrections, execution history, and output conventions. It is durable and governed outside any one model; each task receives only a bounded, permission-safe active context.

This proposal introduces \amos, a model-neutral analytical operating layer between company memory, analytical applications, and the systems they use. \amos externalizes analytical state as typed, versioned memory; constructs task-specific context; plans and executes SQL, Python, statistical, visualization, and reporting work; verifies results; preserves human review; and revisits prior conclusions when governing evidence changes. A model such as Google Gemma 4 may serve as one replaceable execution engine. The model is not the operating system, and conversational data querying---including a Databricks Genie-style experience---is one application on top of \amos rather than the product boundary.

The full product is a platform on which internal teams and external developers build business-review applications, anomaly-response agents, forecasting workflows, planning tools, finance-reporting agents, operational monitors, and vertical analysis systems. The paper-sized research contribution validates its most important substrate: a governed memory layer and analytical state transaction (\atxn) that connect persistent state, bounded context, execution, publication, claim dependencies, replay, and invalidation. We will compare that substrate with matched agents and a production-faithful composed governance stack under controlled schema, metric, data, document, and policy churn, with explicit acceptance and rejection criteria.
\end{abstract}

\section{Motivation and Problem}

An analyst's job is not a single prompt-to-SQL call. It includes understanding a business objective, discovering and assessing data, selecting methods, executing tools, checking uncertainty and quality, producing artifacts, incorporating review, and monitoring for change. \amos is designed to automate this end-to-end loop continuously while retaining the company's accumulated context between tasks.

Correctness depends on more than model reasoning. At time $t$, a task observes independently changing state:
\begin{equation}
  \task_t = (q_t, D_t, C_t, M_t, X_t, H_{<t}, F_{<t}, P_t),
\end{equation}
where $q_t$ is the request, $D_t$ the data snapshot or stream state, $C_t$ schema and catalog state, $M_t$ metric and semantic state, $X_t$ governed documents, $H_{<t}$ prior work, $F_{<t}$ reviewed feedback, and $P_t$ identity and policy.

These systems rarely share a transaction manager. Between retrieval and publication, a metric may change, permission may be revoked, a table may migrate, or a watermark may advance. A fluent answer can therefore be state-invalid even when its SQL ran successfully. Prompt expansion and ordinary retrieval do not solve the time-of-check/time-of-use problem; job-level lineage records execution but not necessarily which claim depended on which governing state.

The research problem is:

\begin{quote}
\emph{Can a governed memory operating layer over heterogeneous analytical state materially reduce state-related failures and audit cost for continuous data analytics agents, compared with conventional agent orchestration and a composed governance stack, without unacceptable latency, false rejection, or operational complexity?}
\end{quote}

The intended output is not only an artifact $Y_t$, but a validated state observation $V_t$, computation $E_t$, claim dependency graph $\prov_t$, and replay descriptor $R_t$:
\begin{equation}
  \task_t \rightarrow (Y_t, V_t, E_t, \prov_t, R_t).
\end{equation}

\section{Product North Star and Platform Boundary}

\subsection{Three Product Identities}

\amos has three identities that share one operating substrate:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Company analytical memory.} A versioned, governed, executable representation of how the company understands its data---not merely a vector database of documents.
  \item \textbf{Data-analysis agent runtime.} The environment for context construction, planning, model invocation, SQL and Python execution, verification, repair, artifact generation, review escalation, memory updates, scheduling, and recovery.
  \item \textbf{Autonomous analytics workforce.} Persistent applications that answer questions, explore data, investigate anomalies, monitor metrics and quality, forecast, evaluate experiments, build reports and dashboards, and update conclusions as evidence changes.
\end{enumerate}

This makes the product category explicit: \amos is the operating layer that turns governed company state into continuously executed, verified, replayable analysis. Models are processors used by the layer; warehouses and lakehouses remain systems of record; user experiences and domain agents are applications above it.

\subsection{The Company's Analytical Constitution}

As \amos observes work and receives governed corrections, it builds a structured analytical constitution. Every learned item carries scope, authority, effective time, permissions, provenance, confidence, and supersession state.

A correction such as ``exclude internal accounts'' is stored with its owner, scope, effective interval, supporting decision, and downstream dependencies so it can affect the next report without silently rewriting unrelated work.

\subsection{Applications Built on AMOS}

Internal teams and external developers build analytical applications against stable memory, execution, verification, and artifact interfaces. Representative applications include business-review apps, anomaly-response agents, forecasting workflows, planning tools, finance-reporting agents, operational monitoring, experiment-analysis systems, and vertical-specific analytical products. These applications share the same company constitution and execution history instead of rebuilding context and governance independently.

A natural-language data assistant can ask questions, generate queries, and explain results. That capability is useful but deliberately subordinate: a Databricks Genie-style interface is one side quest among many. The defining product behavior is that \amos preserves and governs what the company knows, runs analytical work repeatedly, learns from review, and re-evaluates outputs when their dependencies change.

\subsection{Model-Neutral Execution}

\amos may route tasks to different model classes: a small local model for memory classification, an embedding model for entity matching, a coding-capable model for SQL or Python, a reasoning model for investigation planning, a multimodal model for chart inspection, and deterministic code for verification. A customer-hosted Google Gemma 4 model is one possible engine, particularly for controlled or on-premise deployments. Models can be upgraded or replaced without losing metric knowledge, schema mappings, reviewer corrections, execution history, provenance, scheduled work, or policies.

\subsection{Compounding Product Moat}

The durable advantage is not access to a particular model. It compounds through six assets: (1) company-specific analytical memory accumulated across years of definitions, corrections, and decisions; (2) execution history containing successful plans, failed queries, useful segmentations, and reviewer behavior; (3) governed feedback that survives the task in which it was given; (4) an analytical dependency graph linking claims, metrics, data, documents, code, decisions, and reports; (5) connectors that understand versions, permissions, schema change, and source semantics; and (6) a reliable runtime that repeats, recovers, validates, and preserves work across model upgrades.

\subsection{Scope}

The product vision and the research contribution are intentionally different in breadth. The product goal is a complete analytical operating system and application platform. The proposed paper-sized contribution validates its substrate: the typed memory model and the \atxn consistency, context-selection, commit, and invalidation protocol. SQL verification, access control, and provenance storage support that memory contract. The prototype targets read-only analytical SQL, governed documents, charts, and report claims. Arbitrary notebook code, general multi-agent scheduling, autonomous writes to production data, and full LLM trajectory determinism are outside the primary research evaluation, not outside the long-term product.

Raw data remains in its systems of record. \amos stores versioned metadata, compact summaries, policy-safe references, execution records, and dependency edges. It is not a new warehouse or universal enterprise knowledge store.

\section{Startup Build Strategy}

The startup should earn the right to build the full platform through a narrow, measurable sequence. The first commercial wedge is recurring operational analysis for data teams that already have a warehouse, defined business metrics, and painful manual review. AMOS begins as a read-only control layer for one high-value workflow, proves that it prevents stale or unsupported analysis, and then expands into a shared runtime for many analytical applications.

\subsection{Ideal First Customer and Initial Wedge}

The ideal design partner is a 50--500 person software or commerce company with a modern cloud warehouse, a small centralized data team, and at least one recurring metric review or incident workflow. The initial buyer is the head of data or analytics; the daily users are analytics engineers, analysts, and operational leaders. A suitable first workflow runs at least weekly, costs several analyst-hours per cycle, depends on governed metric definitions, and has an identifiable reviewer.

The recommended wedge is a payment-health or subscription-health review. It is frequent, economically meaningful, bounded enough to verify, and already represented by the prototype scenarios. The startup should not begin with unrestricted natural-language access to every company table. It should begin with one approved metric family, one source system, read-only SQL, a small set of report templates, and a mandatory human publication gate.

\subsection{Build Principles}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{One vertical slice before platform breadth.} Complete request-to-report execution for one workflow before adding many connectors or agent types.
  \item \textbf{Governance in the execution path.} Identity, permissions, versions, verification, and provenance are runtime controls, not optional metadata added after generation.
  \item \textbf{Human approval before autonomy.} Early releases recommend, explain, and package evidence. Autonomous publication is enabled only for task classes with measured reliability and an explicit rollback path.
  \item \textbf{Deterministic core, replaceable models.} Policy checks, version validation, storage, and evidence capture stay outside model prompts. Model providers can change without erasing company memory.
  \item \textbf{Every phase has an exit gate.} A phase advances only when customer value, correctness, security, and operability meet predefined thresholds.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Minimum Product Architecture}

The first deployable system has seven services or modules, even if a small team initially packages them as one process: (1) connector adapters that expose source versions and enforce source credentials; (2) a typed memory store; (3) a policy and identity gate; (4) a context compiler; (5) an agent/tool runner; (6) deterministic verification and publication gates; and (7) an evidence store with replay and audit views. A queue supports scheduled work, while an append-only event log supports recovery and debugging. Raw customer data remains in the warehouse; retained samples require explicit policy.

The first production deployment should favor a modular monolith with PostgreSQL, object storage, a managed queue, and isolated tool workers. This keeps transactions and operations understandable for a small team. Service extraction is justified only by tenant isolation, scaling, or deployment requirements. The public contract is the \atxn lifecycle, not the internal service topology.

\subsection{Team, Metrics, and Operating Cadence}

An initial four-to-six person team can cover the wedge: a systems/data engineer for memory and connectors, an agent/runtime engineer, a product engineer for review and audit surfaces, a design-partner lead with analytics depth, and a founder responsible for architecture and customer discovery; security or infrastructure expertise can be fractional until the first paid pilot.

The weekly company scorecard should separate value from safety. Value metrics are analyst-hours saved, time to reviewed answer, weekly completed workflows, reviewer acceptance, and design-partner retention. Safety metrics are state-invalid publication rate, permission incidents, unsupported-claim rate, verifier false rejects, replay success, and mean recovery time. Cost metrics are model cost per accepted artifact, p95 task latency, storage growth, and support hours per account. Growth is not allowed to conceal a regression in permission or publication safety.

Each phase follows the same cadence: discover a customer workflow; freeze its task and policy contract; implement the smallest vertical slice; shadow the existing process; compare results with reviewers; release behind a human gate; instrument failures; and only then generalize reusable primitives. Product requests that do not improve the active phase's exit gate remain out of scope.

\subsection{Commercial and Technical Risks}

The largest commercial risk is becoming a services-heavy analytics consultancy. The mitigation is a fixed onboarding contract, reusable connector interfaces, and a clear limit on customer-specific logic. The largest product risk is a polished assistant without trusted publication behavior; the mitigation is to sell reviewed workflow completion rather than chat. The largest technical risks are source-version ambiguity, verifier false rejection, permission races, and runaway evidence growth. The roadmap measures each explicitly and narrows supported task classes when a safe contract cannot be enforced.

\section{Prior Systems and the Novelty Gap}

\subsection{Agent Context and Memory}

Retrieval-augmented generation supplies non-parametric evidence \cite{rag}; long contexts still require selection and can underuse centrally placed information \cite{lostinthemiddle}. ReAct and tool-using agents interleave model reasoning with actions \cite{react,toolformer}. MemGPT explicitly treats prompt context as managed memory \cite{memgpt}; Generative Agents and Reflexion persist observations or verbal feedback \cite{generativeagents,reflexion}. These systems motivate bounded context and durable memory, but do not provide a cross-source analytical version vector or publish-time validation tied to output claims.

\subsection{Analytical State and Reproducibility}

Stream processors formalize event time, watermarks, state, and replay \cite{millwheel,dataflow,flink}; lakehouse systems provide transactional table versions \cite{deltalake}. Semantic layers, catalogs, and data-quality systems govern definitions and validate data \cite{deequ,tfdv}. Text-to-SQL benchmarks increasingly include enterprise schemas and workflow context \cite{spider,bird,spider2}. These systems govern their own resources, but an agent task can span several of them without a common observation and publication boundary.

\subsection{Provenance, Lineage, and Security}

PROV and OpenLineage model entities, activities, datasets, jobs, and runs \cite{provdm,openlineage}; noWorkflow and YesWorkflow capture script and workflow provenance \cite{noworkflow,yesworkflow}. \amos builds on these representations but proposes a lower-granularity dependency between a published claim and the exact analytical state, computation, and review status that governed it. Indirect prompt injection shows why retrieved content cannot be trusted to enforce its own isolation \cite{greshake2023indirect,yi2023bipia}; \amos therefore places capability authorization outside the model.

\subsection{Why This Is Not Merely Renaming Governance as Memory}

No individual \amos component is claimed as new. The proposed novelty is a unified \emph{memory operating abstraction}: persistent typed analytical memory is projected into bounded active context through a task-scoped transaction that joins (1) the versions and policy under which memory was selected, (2) the capabilities under which tools ran, and (3) the claim dependencies committed at publication. This enables optimistic cross-source validation and claim-level invalidation that ordinary orchestration does not provide automatically.

The decisive comparison is not a weak RAG system. It is a production-faithful stack containing an agent orchestrator, permission-filtered retrieval, semantic definitions, catalog/schema metadata, policy enforcement, OpenLineage-style events, and artifact storage. Both systems receive identical models, tools, sources, budgets, and churn events. The experiment asks whether the explicit \atxn abstraction adds measurable value beyond careful integration.

\section{Technical Core of the AMOS Memory Layer}

\amos separates persistent analytical memory from bounded model-visible context. At time $t$:
\begin{equation}
  \mem_t = (M^d_t, M^c_t, M^m_t, M^x_t, M^h_t, M^f_t, M^p_t),
\end{equation}
where the tiers record data or stream state, schema/catalog state, metric semantics, governed documents, prior analytical history, reviewed feedback, and provenance/policy. Each object carries a stable ID, type, source, authority, effective interval, transaction time, permissions, sensitivity, version, status, supersession edges, and provenance reference. Raw records normally remain in source systems; memory objects hold governed metadata, summaries, and immutable references.

The active context $W_t \subset \mem_t$ is a temporary, task-specific projection constrained by identity, time, required roles, and budget. The \atxn is the privileged protocol by which an agent reads this memory projection, uses scoped tools, validates its observations, and writes artifacts, claims, feedback, and provenance back to persistent memory.

An \atxn is a task-scoped state machine:
\begin{equation}
  A = (u, q, P^0, V^0, W, K, E, C, \prov, o),
\end{equation}
where $u$ is identity, $q$ the task, $P^0$ the policy epoch, $V^0$ a vector of source-version observations, $W$ the bounded working set, $K$ scoped capabilities, $E$ execution records, $C$ extracted or declared claims, $\prov$ typed dependencies, and $o$ the final outcome.

\subsection{Version Vector and Attainable Consistency}

For source systems $s_1,\ldots,s_n$, \amos records:
\begin{equation}
  V^0 = \langle (s_j, v_j, \tau_j, \ell_j) \rangle_{j=1}^{n},
\end{equation}
where $v_j$ is a snapshot, schema, metric, document, or policy version; $\tau_j$ is observation time; and $\ell_j$ is an optional lease or retention deadline. A version is not assumed to be globally simultaneous with other versions.

Each source is assigned one of three declared consistency classes:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{C0 - observed:} a version and observation time are recorded, but the source cannot guarantee retention or monotonic reads.
  \item \textbf{C1 - validated:} the version can be re-read at commit and changes are detectable.
  \item \textbf{C2 - leased or immutable:} the version is retained or leased for the task and can be replayed during the declared interval.
\end{itemize}

Task policy specifies the minimum class for each required role. A high-risk report may require C1 policy, schema, and metric state plus C2 data; a low-risk exploration may tolerate C0 documents with a warning. If a source cannot satisfy the requirement, the runtime does not pretend to have a distributed snapshot: it returns \code{warning}, \code{needs\_review}, or \code{reject}.

\subsection{Transaction Protocol}

\begin{verbatim}
A-TXN(request, identity, policy, budget):
  BEGIN:
    bind identity; read policy epoch P0; collect source vector V0
  ADMIT:
    derive required roles and consistency classes
    reject or downgrade if capabilities, budget, or versions are unavailable
  SELECT:
    filter by policy before candidate ranking
    reconcile logical keys, authority, effective time, and supersession
    choose role-complete working set W within context budget
  EXECUTE:
    issue task-scoped capabilities K
    verify each operation against W and policy; record results E
  VALIDATE:
    re-read policy epoch and all C1 versions; confirm C2 leases
    if a governing version changed: abort, repair, or require review
  COMMIT:
    extract or accept declared claims C; build dependency graph G
    atomically commit artifact, C, G, replay descriptors, and audit events
  INVALIDATE:
    on later source changes, traverse reverse dependencies and revalidate
\end{verbatim}

The linearization point is the successful \code{COMMIT} in the \amos store after external-version validation. This does not make external systems atomic with \amos. It guarantees only that the committed record truthfully names the versions validated at publication and that any detected race prevents a final artifact. Changes after commit are handled by invalidation.

\subsection{Role-complete Bounded Context}

The proposal replaces the undefined goal of a ``sufficient'' context with an operational goal: \emph{role completeness under a declared task schema}. A task parser produces mandatory roles $R(q)$ such as metric, schema, data state, policy, and supporting evidence. Candidate selection solves:
\begin{align}
  \max_{W \subseteq \mem} \quad & \sum_{i \in W}(rel_i + auth_i + time_i + active_i) - \lambda cost_i \\
  \text{s.t.} \quad & permitted(i,P^0) \ \forall i \in W, \\
  & \sum_{i \in W} tokens(i) \leq B, \\
  & \forall r \in R(q),\; \exists i \in W: covers(i,r).
\end{align}

Mandatory-role failure is observable and produces a non-final outcome; it is not hidden by adding more loosely relevant text. Optional evidence fills remaining budget. Retrieval recall is evaluated against independent labels, and omitted candidate counts and uncovered roles are recorded.

\paragraph{Logical identity.} Objects are grouped using source-qualified logical keys issued by an owning connector. Cross-source aliases, renames, merges, and splits require explicit alias or supersession edges with authority and effective intervals. Heuristic matches may propose edges but cannot silently establish governing identity. Multiple approved mappings for the same interval produce a conflict.

\subsection{Bounded Verification Semantics}

The SQL verifier does not claim general query equivalence. It supports a declared fragment: read-only relational SQL with approved aggregates, filters, joins, CTEs, grouping, and a bounded set of window constructs. Queries are parsed into relational expressions and checked against schema and metric constraints. Unsupported dialect features, UDFs, approximate functions, ambiguous NULL semantics, or plans beyond the fragment return \code{needs\_review} or \code{reject} rather than an unsound equivalence claim.

Verifier evaluation measures valid acceptance, invalid rejection, false positive and false negative rates, repair success, and repair-induced violations on an independently authored corpus. The research claim is about useful enforcement within the declared fragment.

\subsection{Claims, Support, and Invalidation}

The unit of publication is a \emph{policy-relevant claim}: a numeric, comparison, causal, forecast, or operational statement whose risk policy requires support or review. Structured reports declare claim spans and types. Free-form artifacts use an extractor with conservative thresholds; high-risk publication requires structured claims or human confirmation. Missed-claim rate is therefore a measured failure mode.

For claim $c$, \amos stores typed edges to computations, data versions, semantic definitions, documents, execution hashes, and review decisions. Three properties remain distinct:
\begin{equation}
  traceable(c) \not\Rightarrow correct(c) \not\Rightarrow causally\_justified(c).
\end{equation}
Provenance establishes traceability. Mechanical verifiers can establish limited computation-to-result consistency. Causal and high-impact operational conclusions remain review-gated unless supported by an approved design.

When source version $v$ is superseded or revoked, reverse traversal finds dependent claims. Each claim is rechecked against its policy: it may remain valid, become stale, be revoked, or require review. The research question is whether this finer invalidation unit reduces review work and missed defects relative to artifact- or job-level lineage.

\subsection{Replay Semantics}

Replay is decomposed into measurable levels:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Computational replay:} rerun deterministic computation against retained inputs; compare result hashes or declared numeric tolerances.
  \item \textbf{Evidence reconstruction:} recover the versions, queries, documents, and review records used at publication.
  \item \textbf{Artifact regeneration:} regenerate charts or structured reports and compare declared semantic fields, not necessarily pixels or prose.
  \item \textbf{Trajectory replay:} reproduce the entire model interaction. This is recorded when possible but is not a core guarantee because providers and sampling can be nondeterministic.
\end{itemize}

An artifact is labeled replayable only at a named level. C2 versions are required for computational replay; C0/C1 sources can still support historical evidence reconstruction.

\subsection{Security Guarantee Boundary}

Retrieved text is treated as untrusted data, but labeling alone is not a security guarantee. Guarantees come from pre-context authorization, tenant-scoped indexes and caches, external capability checks, structured tool APIs, output validation, and commit-time policy revalidation. The model may follow an injected instruction; it still cannot acquire a capability or publish restricted provenance unless the external control plane authorizes it.

The threat model includes malicious retrieved content, malicious authorized users, compromised documents, cross-tenant index leakage, permission-revocation races, provenance expansion, multi-turn poisoning, inference through filtered results, and denial of service. Source-system compromise, aggregate inference below source-enforced privacy thresholds, and side channels outside the measured interfaces are documented residual risks.

\section{Experimental Methodology}

The evaluation is preregistered before external labels or outputs are opened. Independent tasks, annotations, attacks, and human-study records are the statistical units; repeated model samples of the same task are not treated as independent evidence.

The composed stack is implemented before holdout release and reviewed by an engineer not responsible for \amos scoring. It receives explicit orchestration for version reads and artifact storage, so the experiment tests whether the \amos memory layer improves correctness or complexity beyond a credible integration, not whether other tools happen to omit a feature.

\subsection{Independent Task Study}

At least three analysts who did not implement \amos will author 50 tasks in each of three domains, for 150 independent tasks. A different analyst reviews each task for realism and ambiguity; two annotators label required evidence, forbidden evidence, expected SQL or method, state-valid outcome, and review obligations before separate adjudication. Tasks are frozen and hashed before system tuning.

Each task is expressed with three neutral prompt frames and run on three independently frozen model families. The primary paired contrast, \amos versus the matched agent without \amos, requires 2,700 intended executions. Secondary arms run on the same task set under a declared sampling plan. Every intended execution remains in the denominator; provider, timeout, verifier, and malformed-output failures are preserved.

Workloads mix normal analysis with controlled evolution:

\begin{itemize}
  \item schema additions, renames, removals, aliases, joins, and type changes;
  \item metric numerator, denominator, filter, unit, and effective-time changes;
  \item snapshot advancement, watermark lag, late data, retention, and missing partitions;
  \item document supersession, conflicting approvals, and malicious evidence;
  \item permission revocation, role change, policy-cache churn, and provenance expansion;
  \item unchanged benign tasks to measure false warnings, unnecessary review, and utility loss.
\end{itemize}

Churn events occur before selection, during execution, and immediately before commit to exercise the consistency protocol. A task is not designed around \amos terminology and may require completion, clarification, warning, repair, rejection, or human review.

\subsection{Outcomes}

The preregistered primary endpoint is safe-and-correct completion, but it will never be reported alone. The paper will independently report:

\begin{itemize}
  \item analytical answer and SQL correctness;
  \item schema, metric, temporal, and document-state correctness;
  \item permission violations and restricted-evidence exposure;
  \item retrieval recall, role coverage, stale selection, and omitted-evidence errors;
  \item unsupported-claim rate and review-obligation precision/recall;
  \item false rejection, false warning, repair success, and unnecessary-review rates;
  \item computational replay and evidence-reconstruction success;
  \item end-to-end and control-path latency, tokens, execution cost, and storage.
\end{itemize}

The independent task is the primary statistical unit. Paired differences use 10,000-replicate task-cluster bootstrap intervals. A logistic mixed-effects model includes task and author random intercepts and system, model, and prompt fixed effects. Holm correction applies to confirmatory secondary contrasts. Effect sizes, uncertainty intervals, per-task outcomes, and all failures will be released.

\subsection{Verifier Study}

A separately authored 400-query corpus contains 200 valid and 200 invalid cases across aliases, derived columns, CTEs, nested and correlated subqueries, joins, windows, grouping sets, conditional aggregates, NULL behavior, dialects, stale fields, missing metric predicates, comments, obfuscation, writes, DDL, exports, and unsafe functions. Two annotators label each case and adjudicate disagreements.

Primary metrics are valid acceptance, invalid rejection, false positive and false negative rates, repair success, and repair-induced violation rate. Headline use of the verifier requires at least 0.95 valid acceptance, 0.95 invalid rejection, and at most 0.02 repair-induced violations. Failing a construct family narrows the supported SQL fragment; it does not permit task-specific patching after unblinding.

\subsection{Claim and Human Audit Studies}

The claim corpus contains at least 120 raw reports, notebooks, slides, chart annotations, dashboard texts, table cells, and fragments with at least 600 adjudicated claims. Splits are by source artifact and author. Regex, prompted LLM, structured-output LLM, and a competitive supervised model are compared on exact-span F1, claim-type macro-F1, review-obligation F1, unsupported-claim F1, and evidence-link F1.

A within-participant, randomized, counterbalanced audit study recruits at least 36 technically qualified participants. Participants inspect seeded reports under either claim-level dependencies or ordinary citations plus job logs. Primary outcomes are defect-detection rate and audit time; secondary outcomes are false alarms, confidence, replay success, and usability. H3 requires improvement in at least one primary outcome without credible degradation in the other.

\subsection{Security Study}

At least three independent red-teamers create 120 attacks across direct and indirect prompt injection, cross-user leakage, malicious authoritative sources, permission revocation, provenance expansion, timing or filtered-result inference, and multi-turn memory poisoning. Protected assets, attacker knowledge, allowed credentials, and success criteria are frozen in advance.

The primary security outcome is attack success rate, paired with safe-and-correct completion on matched benign tasks. A confirmed permission leak fails the headline security gate. The study distinguishes model compromise from system compromise: an injected model response counts as contained if external capabilities and publication gates prevent unauthorized effects.

\subsection{Systems, Scale, Churn, and Recovery}

Systems experiments use 10,000, 100,000, and 1,000,000 memory objects with matched provenance graphs, plus sensitivity runs at higher fan-out where hardware permits. Workloads vary context budget, candidate engine, 1-64 concurrent readers, mixed reads/writes, policy and schema churn, invalidation fan-out, cache state, source delay, connector failure, and store restart.

Measurements include p50/p95/p99 retrieval and transaction latency, throughput, CPU, memory, storage amplification, token use, cost, stale-commit rate, version-validation aborts, retry amplification, invalidation completion, recovery time, and correctness after recovery. H4 fails if \amos exceeds 25\% matched control-path overhead without a significant state-validity or audit benefit, or if correctness drops by more than five points as persistent state scales while context budget remains fixed.

\subsection{Ablations}

The ablation matrix removes one mechanism at a time:

\begin{itemize}
  \item no publish-time version or policy revalidation;
  \item no effective-time or authority reconciliation;
  \item post-retrieval permission filtering instead of index pushdown;
  \item unconstrained top-$k$ retrieval instead of mandatory role coverage;
  \item artifact-level lineage instead of claim dependencies;
  \item no reverse invalidation traversal;
  \item C0 observation only instead of C1/C2 consistency classes.
\end{itemize}

The purpose is to identify which part of \atxn causes any observed benefit. If the full system improves but no mechanism explains the effect, the proposal will report the result as unresolved rather than attributing it to the abstraction.

\section{Expected Contributions}

The project is expected to contribute:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{A memory-layer abstraction:} persistent typed analytical memory, bounded active-context projections, and explicit read/write authority for continuous data analytics agents.
  \item \textbf{A transaction protocol:} \atxn, with consistency classes, optimistic version validation, capability-scoped execution, and a publication linearization point.
  \item \textbf{Two coupled memory mechanisms:} role-complete bounded context selection and claim-level dependency/invalidation semantics, both evaluated independently through ablations.
  \item \textbf{A reference implementation and composed baseline:} matched enough to test whether unification adds value beyond ordinary integration.
  \item \textbf{Independent evidence:} task, verifier, claim, human-audit, red-team, and scaling studies with preregistered failure criteria.
  \item \textbf{A reusable artifact:} schemas and tools for measuring state-related failures in long-running analytical agents, even if the central hypothesis is rejected.
\end{enumerate}

The strongest possible result is not that \amos passes its own invariants. It is that the memory layer produces a reproducible improvement over a credible composed alternative and that the improvement can be attributed to its context, transaction, and dependency mechanisms at measured cost. A null result is also informative: it would show that conventional composition is sufficient or that the proposed safety-utility tradeoff is not worthwhile.

\section{Conclusion}

\amos is designed to become the persistent analytical intelligence of a company: an operating layer in company memory that understands its data, metrics, ontology, policies, history, authority, decisions, and preferred ways of communicating, then continuously performs the complete work of data analysis. Teams and external developers build analytical applications on that shared substrate. Conversational querying is one such application, and a model such as Google Gemma 4 is one replaceable processor; neither defines the product boundary.

The research program deliberately narrows this broad product vision to a falsifiable systems question. \amos keeps persistent typed analytical state outside the model, constructs bounded governed context for each task, and preserves corrections and evidence across tasks. \atxn makes the layer enforceable through version vectors, role-complete context selection, scoped capabilities, optimistic commit validation, and claim dependencies.

The research program is designed to reject the idea if it does not outperform a production-faithful composed stack, if its verifier rejects valid work, if its audit interface does not help reviewers, if its security boundary leaks, or if its overhead overwhelms the benefit. That combination of mechanism, comparison, and failure criteria is the basis on which \amos should be judged as systems research.
